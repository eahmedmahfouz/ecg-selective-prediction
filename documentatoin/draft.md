Idead 1: Risk-controlled clinical triage via conformal prediction on long-duration ambulatory ECG
The gap. The single biggest mismatch between the Hannun model and a deployable clinical product is the absence of guarantees. Cardiologist over-readers spend most of their time on Zio reports rubber-stamping nothing-burgers; the value proposition of a model is triage, not classification. But triage requires a guarantee: "if I send these N% of records straight through, I will miss at most X% of, say, sustained VT episodes." The current model gives you a probability that nobody can interpret as a frequentist coverage statement.
The novelty. Conformal prediction in medical AI is an active area, but applications to long-duration, time-series, multi-class ECG triage with rhythm-specific risk control (rule-out vs. rule-in asymmetry) are sparse. Most conformal-medical work is on single-image classification with i.i.d. assumptions — Zio data violates exchangeability across time within a patient and across patients in a deployment cohort.
Concrete formulation. Train a model (you can literally start with the awni/ecg checkpoint) and then build a calibration layer on top:

Use Learn-then-Test or risk-controlling prediction sets (Angelopoulos & Bates) with rhythm-specific loss functions — a missed run of VT is not the same as a missed bigeminy beat.
Address temporal non-exchangeability with weighted conformal prediction or recent online conformal methods (Gibbs & Candès), since a multi-day record is one long sequence per patient.
Output two artifacts: (a) a calibrated episode-level prediction set with finite-sample coverage, and (b) a triage decision (route to cardiologist, auto-confirm, or reject as noise) with explicit FNR control on actionable rhythms.

Evaluation. Beyond AUC/F1, report: (i) average review-time saved at fixed FNR, (ii) coverage on subgroups (age, sex, device-noise level — the paper explicitly couldn't do subgroup analysis), (iii) coverage under simulated covariate shift (e.g., calibrate on Zio, deploy on a 12-lead test set or PhysioNet Challenge data — both publicly available).

 Idea 2: Selective prediction and calibrated abstention for multi-label ECG classification
The gap. Almost every PTB-XL paper reports AUC and F1. Almost none report calibration error, and none I'm aware of seriously study selective prediction — the operational question of "if the model can route X% of the hardest cases to a cardiologist, how much does the error rate on the remaining cases drop, and can we guarantee that?" The Strodthoff benchmark touched on uncertainty but didn't formalize abstention. The clinical translation gap is enormous: a model that says "I'm 0.62 confident this is AFib" is useless without knowing whether that 0.62 is actually 62% reliable.
The multi-label setting (PTB-XL records can have several co-occurring statements) makes this genuinely under-studied — most selective-prediction theory is single-label. That's a real methodological hole.
Why this is publishable. It addresses a stated need in the clinical-AI literature ("human-in-the-loop deployment"), it's reproducible because PTB-XL is fully open, and it produces a deliverable other researchers want: calibrated checkpoints + a benchmark protocol. JAMIA Open, Computers in Biology and Medicine, IEEE JBHI all publish this kind of work. The position paper I found above is essentially asking for exactly this kind of contribution.
Concrete plan.
Dataset. PTB-XL at 100 Hz (~1.7 GB), the standard 5 superdiagnostic-class task plus the 23-class subdiagnostic task and the 12-class rhythm task. All three are in the Strodthoff benchmark so you have direct comparators. Optional generalization test: CPSC2018 and Chapman-Shaoxing for cross-dataset evaluation (both public, both laptop-sized).
Models. Don't try to invent a new architecture — use the two best from the Strodthoff benchmark (xresnet1d101 and inception1d, both <5M params). Keep architecture fixed; vary the post-hoc calibration and abstention layer.
Methods to compare. Temperature scaling, vector scaling, isotonic regression, Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles (5 seeds — laptop can do this), evidential deep learning, and conformal risk control for the multi-label case (this is where the methodological contribution sits — adapting Angelopoulos' framework to multi-label ECG with class-conditional risk).
Evaluation. Risk-coverage curves, expected calibration error (ECE) and adaptive ECE per class, conditional coverage on subgroups (age bins, sex), and a clinical-utility metric: at fixed sensitivity for actionable rhythms (AFib, AVB, VT), how much specificity gain do you buy by abstaining on N% of records?
